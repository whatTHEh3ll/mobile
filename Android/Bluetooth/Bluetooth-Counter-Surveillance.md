## Summary

- Your devices constantly emit radio signals that can be tracked, from smartphones to IoT gadgets.
- Apple iPhones, AirTags, and similar devices broadcast Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) announcements every few seconds, leaking location data.
- This signal leakage enables "counter‑surveillance": you can locate and track those who might be tracking you.
- BLE’s range can reach up to 200 m (≈ 600 ft) outdoors, far surpassing classic Bluetooth’s ~10 m range.
- The underlying mesh networks (Apple AirTag, Amazon Sidewalk, Google Mesh) rely on peer‑to‑peer BLE communication.
- Apps like “BLE Radar” let Android users scan, identify, and log nearby BLE devices, exposing MAC addresses, headings, and inferred device types.
- Counter‑measures include using de‑Googled phones, disabling BLE when not needed, and tagging known devices to filter out clutter.
- Awareness of BLE tracking empowers users to reduce their radio emission profile and protect privacy.

## Action Items / Takeaways

- [ ] Audit your devices: identify which emit BLE signals (phones, trackers, IoT appliances).
- [ ] Install a BLE scanning app (e.g., BLE Radar) on Android to detect nearby trackers.
- [ ] Consider using a de‑Googled or privacy‑focused phone to minimize BLE emissions.
- [ ] Disable BLE or location services when not required, especially on wearable devices.
- [ ] Tag known devices (e.g., home appliances) to filter out clutter in scan results.
- [ ] Stay informed about mesh network services (AirTag, Sidewalk, Google Mesh) and their privacy implications.
- [ ] Support privacy‑first communities and tools that help reclaim radio‑signal control.
